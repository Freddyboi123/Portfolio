Env and dev security

1 April 2026·435 words·3 mins
Author
Frederik Edvardsen

There are many aspects of security that we, as developers, must consider. Some of the security measures we implement in our systems may initially appear unnecessary to the untrained developer .

This was the impression I had when we began implementing .env files in our program. My initial thought was: - why is it necessary to hide information in a program that I alone am working on?.

However, as I continued working, I began to question this assumption. Is this truly my project alone? Am I the only individual with access to it? While the answer may appear to be yes, this perspective does not fully reflect the reality of a typical development workflow.

In practice, a project is rarely isolated. Multiple individuals may review or interact with the codebase over time. In my case, this includes my study group, with whom I collaborate regularly, sharing progress, challenges, and solutions.

Therefore, security considerations should not be limited solely to protection against external threats. Internal exposure—intentional or unintentional—must also be taken into account.

lets showcase an examble.

real world examble
#

When i was working on my deployment pipeline.

i ran into a problem with my docker-compose.yml, and i asked my study gruop for help. this is was they saw.

  db:
    image: postgres:16.2
    container_name: db
    restart: unless-stopped
    environment:
      POSTGRES_USER: someUsername
      POSTGRES_PASSWORD: securePassword
    volumes:
      - ./postgres_data:/var/lib/postgresql/data/
      - ./db/init.sql:/docker-entrypoint-initdb.d/init.sql
    ports:
      - "5432:5432"
    networks:
      - backend

As shown above, my private data is directly visible in the configuration file.

Even though I trust the people I shared this with and know they have no ill intentions, this situation highlights an important principle. In programming, and especially in security, it is important to follow a “better safe than sorry” approach.

Sensitive information should not be exposed unnecessarily, regardless of the context. Using tools like .env files helps ensure that credentials are kept separate from the codebase and reduces the risk of accidental exposure.

By setting up a .env file on our ubuntoserver, we can get around this problem.

ISSUER="Frederik Edvardsen"
TOKEN_EXPIRE_TIME=1800000
SECRET_KEY=12345678901234567890123456789012
DB_USERNAME="someUsername"
DB_PASSWORD="securePassword"
DEPLOYED="true"
CONNECTION_STR="jdbc:postgresql://db:5432/"
DB_NAME="vores"

This allows us to store sensitive information outside of our main configuration files.

As a result, we no longer have to expose credentials directly in our docker-compose.yml. Instead, we can reference environment variables like this:

  db:
    image: postgres:16.2
    container_name: db
    restart: unless-stopped
    environment:
      POSTGRES_USER: ${DB_USERNAME}
      POSTGRES_PASSWORD: ${DB_PASSWORD}
    volumes:
      - ./postgres_data:/var/lib/postgresql/data/
      - ./db/init.sql:/docker-entrypoint-initdb.d/init.sql
    ports:
      - "5432:5432"
    networks:
      - backend

By separating sensitive data from the codebase, we reduce the risk of accidentally exposing credentials when sharing or collaborating on the project. This approach follows the general principle of minimizing unnecessary exposure of confidential information.
